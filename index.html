Socrates

For to fear death, gentlemen, is nothing else than to think one is wise when one is not; for it is thinking one knows what one does not know. For no one knows whether death be not even the greatest of all blessings to man, but they fear it as if they knew that it is the greatest of evils.

-Socrates / Apology--Plato

A Life Examined

Who was Socrates?

Socrates was born 469BC in Athens and died in 399 BCE. He was an ancient Greek philosopher. His lifestyle, character, and thoughts have had a profound impact on both ancient and modern philosophy.

Since Socrates did not write anything, our understanding of his personality and teachings primarily comes from the dialogues of Plato, the Memorabilia of Xenophon, and various writings of Aristotle. Socrates was a brave soldier in the Peloponnesian War and later served in the Athenian assembly.

Socrates believed it was his religious duty to encourage his fellow citizens to lead an examined life by engaging them in philosophical conversations. His contribution to these discussions often involved a series of probing questions that gradually exposed his conversation partner's complete lack of knowledge on the subject. This teaching technique is known as the "Socratic method."

Despite often claiming ignorance on many subjects he explored, mainly ethical ones, Socrates held certain beliefs with conviction. These included the idea that human wisdom begins with recognizing one's own ignorance, that an unexamined life is not worth living, that ethical virtue is the only thing that matters, and that a good person can never be harmed because their virtue remains intact regardless of misfortune.

Among his students and admirers were Plato, Alcibiades, who betrayed Athens in the Peloponnesian War, and Critias, one of the Thirty Tyrants imposed on Athens after its defeat by Sparta. Socrates' association with these men, along with his habit of exposing the ignorance of his fellow citizens, made him widely disliked and feared. As a result, Socrates was tried on charges of impiety and corrupting the youth and was sentenced to death by poisoning (likely hemlock) in 399 BCE, a sentence he accepted willingly.

Year

Life Event
c. 470 BCE Socrates is born in Athens, Greece, to Sophroniscus, a stonemason, and Phaenarete, a midwife.

May. Socrates is sentenced to death by drinking a cup of poison hemlock. He accepts his sentence with calmness and dies in the presence of his friends, including Plato.
After 399 BCE Plato and other students of Socrates write dialogues featuring their teacher as the central character, preserving his philosophical ideas and methods for future generations.

The Death Of Socrates

The Story

Plato's Apology is believed to be the speech Socrates gave in his defense. As portrayed in the Apology, Socrates' trial and death raise important questions about democracy, the value of free speech, and the potential conflict between moral and religious obligations and the laws of the state.

In Plato’s Apology(29a-b), Socrates agues that he does not fear death; indeed, to fear death is a sign of ignorance. It is to claim to know what one in fact does not know (Ap. 29 a-b). Perhaps, Socrates suggests, death is not a great evil after all, but "the greatest of all goods.” At the end of the dialogue, after the judges have voted on the final verdict and Socrates has received the death penalty, the philosopher considers two common views of death: that death is a long dreamless sleep and that death is a journey to another place - Hades. According to Socrates, either of these views of death would be acceptable to him; the one, because he would receive a wonderful rest with no dreams to disturb him; the other, because he would be able to talk philosophy with those who had gone before with impunity.

The Paintings

The Death of Socrates (French: La Mort de Socrate) is an oil on canvas painted by French painter Jacques-Louis David in 1787. The painting was part of the neoclassical style, popular in the 1780s, that depicted subjects from the Classical age, in this case the story ofthe execution of Socrates as told by Plato in his Phaedo. In this story, Socrates has been convicted of corrupting the youth of Athens and introducing strange gods, and has been sentenced to die by drinking poison hemlock. Socrates uses his death as a final lesson for his pupils rather than fleeing when the opportunity arises, and faces it calmly. The Phaedo depicts the death of Socrates and is also Plato's fourth and last dialogue to detail the philosopher's final days, which is also detailed in Euthyphro, Apology, and Crito.
